# Bloom & Burrow (antgame)
Two player asymmetric, cozy, seasonal city building

## How it plays
Two players share one ant colony through separate screens, joined by a single **dock**. Everything one player gathers, the other must put to use — neither can win alone.

**Bloom** tends the meadow above. You paint living pheromone trails from the pit out to flowers (sugar) and carcasses (protein). Trails fade over time but are kept alive by the ants that walk them, so a busy network sustains itself while neglected routes wither. Trails branch into a shared tree, and ants carry one of each food at once — so a single clever route threaded through both a flower and a carcass outhauls two separate lines. Distant sources are richest, trading a long permanent trunk against cheap trails you keep redrawing.

**Burrow** shapes the nest below. You dig tunnels and chambers against water that genuinely flows — rain pours in through the entrance, pooling and spreading through whatever you carved, so sumps, drainage shafts, and sealable gates are the difference between a dry colony and a drowned queen. You set how many workers forage versus stay home, stockpile food, and raise brood. The queen is a piece you move: eggs only mature in her warmth, so she rides up to the nursery in the growing seasons and down into a deep bunker each winter as frost creeps through the ceiling.

Both worlds run on one seasonal clock. **Spring** melt and **autumn** storms flood the deep; **summer** droughts bake the surface trails; **winter** is a frozen survival sprint on stored reserves. Come through it and the colony scores another year — and the next one hits harder. Lose the queen to flood, frost, or starvation and it ends.

## Target group:
- Couples / Friends that want to build something enjoyable with depth together
- Niche: Cozy x Coop x Colony

## The players two worlds connected by dock:
Outside / *Bloom* (Player 1):
- landscape architect (farming, terrain, and routes) 
- verbs: scout, route workers, allocate, assign, herd, mob, recall
- structural, expansive, exploratory
- Invests labor into foraging and farming to have more resources later.
- UI: birds eye map with protein and nektar sources, water holes, plants
- structures added by ants: pheromone trails, drainage channels, shading structures, planted seeds

Inside / *Burrow* (Player 2):
- quatermaster (ratios, brood timing, rationing, economics)
- verbs: hoard, route resources, dig, shelter, breed, tend queen
- allocative, resilient, engine building
- Invests labor into breeding to have more workforce later.
- UI: vertical cut through the anthill with different tunnels and chambers
- structures: Nursery, Laboratory, Stockpiles, tunnels, funghus plantation

## The development stages (can also be researched in different order by Burrow):
1. *Minors*: the all-purpose worker. Foraging, transporting sugar and protein, brood care, fungus tending. (The whole game in miniature: just minors and the dock.)
2. *Two foods*: protein vs. sugar split, the first inbound tradeoff.
3. *Bigs*: draft animals, individually controllable. Haul whole carcasses, crack tough seeds, dig chambers, transports aphids, cuts leaves. Introduces the outbound caste-ratio bet (every big is raised instead of several minors and eats more. Muscle traded for throughput) and deployment (which heavy job, which herd to guard).
4. *Aphid* dairy: Scout and found aphid colonies on leaves. The renewable, efficient but fragile nektar source. Little bit nomads, walk the herd to fresh stems when one is tapped out. Eggs must be stored underground over winter or when herd is killed by flood / heatwave.
5. *Repletes*: living storage units and more efficient transport vehicles.
6. *Fungus* farming: the leafcutting production line. Plant and harvest leaves that can be chewed up and used to farm funghi under ground as a renewable protein source.

## The seasons:
*Spring*: Wake, Build up, favorable for reproduction
- Friendly sun
- Bloom: Forage carcasses, Build up agriculture infrastructure, harvest some leaves
- Burrow: Floods from below, can be shielded. 

*Summer*: Full swing, most efficient for workers and farming
- Sun. Maybe some droughts that can evaporate trails or kill crop if not sheltered from sun. Burrow can dry out from above, 
- Bloom: Nurish and protect Animals from sun, maybe give them water, dig trenches to prepare for the flood.
- Burrow: Build water ecosystem against drought. Cultivate funghi, do research to advance in Stages

*Autumn*: Pull back, sources die and get risky, Population halves if large,
- Weather: Rain. Floods might destroy the fields and remove the trails. Ground level might raise during floods
- Bloom: Save as many resources as possible from peril, bring home the Aphid eggs
- Burrow: Fill up the stocks, reduce ant production, pull together the colony around the queen to warm her up or dig a deep bunker deep where there is no frost

*Winter*: Survival, Frost, is skipped / fast forwarded. Colony might die here or is decimated

## Decison points / loops the players are optimizing:
- Farm Sugar vs. Protein. Hoard or spend it
- What Labor to produce (Minor, Big, Storage) and where to use it
- What to research next
- Each one individually: How to protect colony against nature

## What's fun:
- systems-humming aesthetic pleasure
- Triumph over world of challenges
- complementary skill building, acting on partners forecasts

## Fail States:
- Game end: Queen flooded (either via dock or from below)/ contaminated (funghus entered via goods from dock and spreaded inside the colony) / dried out (best would be if also both have to fail to loose the queen)
- setback events:
- Bloom: Structures flooded, crop dry, pheromone trails evaporated/flushed away. Workers die of cold. Aphids die. Workers don't have sugar on their trips.
- Burrow: Flooding from below, Freezing or drying out from above, funghus contamination, larvaes don't have proteins, workers don't have sugar
- Mismanagement: Dock is jammed, Workers misallocated between inside / outside, not enough Bigs or Repletes or they are mismanaged, Not enough protein/sugar, No shadow / Aphid leaves dry out, not enough cooling water, no plantations to have new leaves growing, not enough workers taking care of brood, starvation due to not enough stocks for winter, overcrowding: too big colony compared to how much space it has makes half of the colony swarm
